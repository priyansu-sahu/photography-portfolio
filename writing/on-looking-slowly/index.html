on looking slowly

Looking slowly begins before the camera is raised. It is the small pause between noticing a scene and deciding what it might mean: the line of a path, a person crossing into light, a horizon that keeps changing even when it appears still.

Most places do not announce themselves all at once. They reveal their rhythm through repeated glances. A street corner becomes less about buildings and more about crossings. A cliff becomes less about scale and more about the weather moving over it.

The camera helps, but it also interrupts. It asks for an edge, a frame, a choice. The work is to keep that choice gentle enough that the place remains larger than the photograph.

When a frame works, it feels less like taking something and more like staying with it long enough to be allowed a record. The image is not proof of having been there. It is a small note from the act of paying attention.

That is the kind of looking I want to practice: slower than habit, quieter than spectacle, and patient enough to let ordinary light become memorable.
